When we shine light through a prism, it is broken into its component parts and we would see a spectrum.  There are three different types of spectra (see Figure F-2-3):

1. Continuous spectrum – produced by a hot and dense energy source. Examples are cores of stars, incandescent light bulbs, any opaque objects that have some temperature. In Astro 1 we referred to these objects as having thermal radiation which we represented by blackbody curves. From the figure, you can see that a continuous spectrum can be described as all colors of the rainbow, or a black body curve.  

2. Emission spectrum – produced by gases and transparent objects.  Examples are gaseous nebula in the Milky Way galaxy, a neon light bulb, or any excited gas.  Emission spectrum consists of bright color emission lines overlaid on a black background.

3. Absorption spectrum – is a combination of the previous two.  We see an absorption spectrum when we are looking at a continuous source through some gas that is cooler than the continuous source.  See Figure c bellow.  A real life example is a spectrum of any star.  Stars have hot dense cores that produce uninterrupted continuous spectra but their photospheres (upper layers) consist of a cooler gas that absorbs some of the light from the core. This absorbed light looks like dark lines on top of a continuous spectrum.
