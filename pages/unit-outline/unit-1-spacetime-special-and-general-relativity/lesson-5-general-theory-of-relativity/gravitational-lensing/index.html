Curved spacetime alters the paths of light rays, shifting the apparent positions of objects in an effect called gravitational lensing. Gravitational lensing can distort the images of objects.

Lensing can even make one object appear to be at two or more points in the sky.

F-5-8
Lensing

When the deflecting body is massive enough, then the light deflection can work like a lens and produce images of the lensed object so that it:

  1.  has multiple images (Einstein ring, Einstein cross)
  2.  has long arcs; and
  3.  is magnified in both size and brightness

When four images of a lensed object appear in the sky we call this Einstein’s Cross. (see Figure). A ring of light from the background object appears in the sky is called Einstein’s Ring.

Einsteins' Cross and Einstein's Ring

On the left, you see the Einstein Cross that consists of four images of the same supernova that exploded over 9 billion years ago. On the right hand figure, you can see how gravity of a foreground elliptical galaxy (center) bends light from an object almost directly behind it. This gravitational lensing configuration is called the Einstein’s Ring.

You can try to simulate gravitational lensing on your own, using a wine glass and a background light (see figure).
